[[Learnings for literature review]]

### 1) Stop sub-vocalizing (a little)

Most people “say” the words in their head. That caps speed.

- **Don’t try to eliminate it completely** (it’s useful for tricky text), but reduce it.
    
- Try reading while doing a light “mouth busy” task: gently hum, count “1-2-3-4” in your head, or tap a finger rhythm. This makes it harder to internally pronounce every word.
    

### 2) Use a pointer (pacer)

Your eyes naturally wander and backtrack. A pointer forces smoother movement.

- Use your finger, a pen tip, or your cursor.
    
- Move it **slightly faster than comfortable** and let your eyes follow.
    
- Aim for steady motion, not jumping back to re-read.
    

### 3) Read in “chunks,” not single words

Train yourself to take in **2–5 words at a glance**.

- Start by consciously grouping: “in the morning / we went to / the market”
    
- Your peripheral vision can capture more than you think—chunking is where most speed gains come from.
    

### 4) Reduce regressions (unnecessary re-reading)

A lot of slow reading is “rewind habits,” not difficulty.

- Make a rule: **don’t go back unless you truly lost the thread**.
    
- If a sentence doesn’t land, keep going to the end of the paragraph first; often context fixes it.
    

### 5) Match the method to the material

Speed reading is easiest on straightforward prose; harder on dense technical stuff.

- **Skim strategically** for structure (headers, topic sentences) then slow down only where needed.
    
- For textbooks: preview → read → quick recall.
